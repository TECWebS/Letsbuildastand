1
Assess & prep the camper
Before buying anything — inspect and measure
Start here
  • Pop the camper top to full open height and secure the lift mechanism with locking pins or a 2x4 prop
  • Measure interior width, depth, and height — draw a simple sketch with exact dimensions
  • Remove canvas tent fabric from the pop-out bed ends (keep the hardware in case you need it)
  • Inspect the aluminum frame rails for rust or damage — wire brush and treat with Rust-Oleum if needed
  • Identify where the roof lifts from (usually a center crank) — decide if the roof will be fixed permanently or remain operable
  • Check the floor for soft spots — push hard with your foot in all corners. Replace any rotted OSB before framing walls
2
Lock the roof in place
Permanent or semi-permanent — your choice
Phase 2
  • Crank the roof to your desired height (typically 6.5–7 ft for a comfortable serving space)
  • Cut four 2x4 vertical corner supports to match the exact height from floor to roof edge
  • Attach corner posts to the existing aluminum frame using 3/8" carriage bolts with washers and locking nuts — drill through the aluminum
  • Add a 2x4 top plate along all four sides to tie the posts together and give the roof something solid to rest on
  • Apply foam weatherstrip tape between the roof edge and the top plate to seal gaps
3
Frame the walls
The skeleton everything attaches to
Phase 3
  • Use 2x2 or 2x3 lumber for interior wall framing — lighter than 2x4 and sufficient since you're not load-bearing
  • Frame the back wall first as a full solid panel — attach studs every 16" between the corner posts
  • Frame the two side walls, leaving a rough opening on the service side for your window/counter (typically 48–60" wide x 36" tall)
  • Frame a header beam above the service window opening using doubled 2x3 to carry the load
  • Attach framing to the camper's existing aluminum channels using 1/4" lag screws with aluminum-rated washers
  • Add blocking (small horizontal 2x2 pieces) between studs every 24" for PVC plank backing
4
Install PVC wall planks
Your white beaded tongue & groove panels
Phase 4
  • Start at the bottom of each wall — cut the first plank to length with a miter saw or fine-tooth handsaw
  • Fasten planks to framing using 1-5/8" stainless finish nails (nail gun) or white-head trim screws — nail through the tongue so the groove hides the fastener
  • Work upward course by course — tap each tongue into the groove with a rubber mallet to avoid cracking
  • Cut around any camper features (lights, door hinges) with a jigsaw
  • Apply white PVC trim at all corners using PVC corner molding — glue with PVC cement, then nail
  • Caulk all seams, corners, and the roof edge with white paintable exterior caulk
5
Build the service window & counter
The heart of your food cart look
Phase 5
  • Cut a panel of 3/4" marine-grade plywood for the fold-down counter top — size it 6–8" wider than the window opening
  • Attach the counter with a 72" continuous (piano) hinge along the bottom of the window opening
  • Add two folding shelf support brackets (Stanley or Everbilt) on each side to hold the counter when open
  • Cover the counter top with a food-safe butcher block adhesive wrap or seal plywood with 3 coats of exterior polyurethane
  • Build the fold-up awning panel from 1/4" plywood or corrugated PVC — attach with a second piano hinge at top of window, hold open with chain or gas struts
  • Add string lights along the awning interior using adhesive clips and an outdoor-rated extension cord
6
Finishing & branding
Make it look like your vision image
Phase 6
  • Prime all wood surfaces exposed to weather with exterior primer — PVC planks don't need paint but wood trim does
  • Paint exterior wood trim with exterior latex — 2 coats minimum
  • Apply your business name decal or painted lettering to the upper fascia panel (between roof edge and window)
  • Install exterior hooks or brackets for chalkboard signs on each side of the window
  • Add interior shelving using 1x8 pine boards on shelf pin strip tracks — adjustable height
  • Install small LED puck lights inside upper shelf area for warm display lighting

Weight distribution
PVC planks are light, which is great — but your 2x4 corner posts add up. Keep heavy framing as close to the trailer axle as possible to avoid tongue-heavy or tail-heavy towing issues.
PVC plank cutting tip
Use a fine-tooth blade (80-tooth) on your circular saw — PVC melts and gums up coarse blades. Cut slowly and let the blade do the work.
Don't skip the blocking
PVC planks will bow and look wavy without solid backing every 16–24 inches. Add horizontal blocking between studs even where you don't think you need it.
Service window height
Standard food cart counter height is 34–36 inches from the ground. Measure your team's comfortable serving height before cutting the window opening.
Massachusetts permit heads-up
Even for a serving-only cart, Worcester requires a Temporary Food Establishment permit from the Board of Health. Apply early — it can take 4–6 weeks.
Seal everything before it rains
Do the caulking phase in a single session before the next rain. Any exposed wood in the frame that gets wet before planking is installed can warp and cause alignment issues.
Do a dry fit first
Before nailing any PVC planks, do a full dry-fit of an entire wall to confirm your spacing and cuts. It's much easier to adjust before you've fastened anything.
