Build Phases

Work through each phase in order. Click any phase to expand it, then check off steps as you complete them.

1
Assess & Prep the Camper
Before buying anything — inspect, clean, and measure
Start Here 0/6
▾
0%
  • Pop the camper top to full open height and secure the lift mechanism with locking pins or a 2×4 prop
  • Measure interior width, depth, and height — draw a simple sketch with exact dimensions
  • Remove canvas tent fabric from the pop-out bed ends (keep all hardware in a labeled bag)
  • Inspect the aluminum frame rails for rust — wire brush and treat with Rust-Oleum Rust Converter if needed
  • Decide: will the roof be fixed permanently or remain operable? This affects everything that follows
  • Check the floor for soft spots by pushing hard with your foot in every corner — replace rotted OSB before framing
2
Lock the Roof in Place
Set your permanent height — typically 6.5–7 ft
Phase 2 0/5
▾
0%
  • Crank the roof to your desired height and temporarily prop it solid while you work
  • Cut four 2×4 vertical corner posts to match the exact height from floor to roof edge
  • Attach corner posts to the existing aluminum frame using 3/8" carriage bolts with washers and locking nuts — drill through the aluminum
  • Add a 2×4 top plate along all four sides to tie the posts together and give the roof a solid seat
  • Apply foam weatherstrip tape between the roof edge and the top plate to seal out rain
3
Frame the Walls
The skeleton everything attaches to
Phase 3 0/6
▾
0%
  • Use 2×2 or 2×3 lumber for wall framing — lighter than 2×4 and sufficient since you're not load-bearing
  • Frame the back wall first as a full solid panel with studs every 16" between corner posts
  • Frame the two side walls, leaving a rough opening on the service side: typically 48–60" wide × 36" tall for the window
  • Frame a header beam above the service window using doubled 2×3 to carry the load above the opening
  • Attach framing to the camper's aluminum channels using 1/4" lag screws with aluminum-rated washers
  • Add horizontal blocking between studs every 24" to give PVC planks solid backing and prevent bowing
4
Install PVC Wall Planks
White beaded tongue & groove — your signature look
Phase 4 0/6
▾
0%
  • Start at the bottom of each wall — dry-fit an entire wall before nailing anything to confirm spacing
  • Fasten planks to framing with 1-5/8" stainless finish nails through the tongue — this hides the fastener in the next groove
  • Work upward course by course — tap each tongue into the groove with a rubber mallet, never a metal hammer
  • Cut around camper features (lights, door, hinges) using a jigsaw with a fine-tooth blade
  • Apply white PVC corner molding at all inside and outside corners — use PVC cement and finish nails
  • Caulk all seams, corners, and the roof/wall junction with white paintable exterior caulk in one continuous session
5
Build the Service Window & Counter
The fold-down counter and awning — the heart of your cart
Phase 5 0/6
▾
0%
  • Cut 3/4" marine-grade plywood for the fold-down counter — 6–8" wider than the window opening, depth of 18–24"
  • Attach the counter with a 72" continuous (piano) hinge along the bottom edge of the window opening
  • Install two Everbilt heavy-duty folding shelf support brackets on each side to hold the counter level when open
  • Seal counter top with 3 coats of exterior polyurethane or cover with food-safe butcher block adhesive wrap
  • Build the fold-up awning panel from 1/4" plywood — attach with a piano hinge at the top of the window, hold open with chain or gas struts
  • Run string lights along the awning underside using adhesive clips and an outdoor-rated extension cord
6
Finishing & Branding
Paint, signage, shelving, and lights
Final Phase 0/6
▾
0%
  • Prime all exposed wood trim and the awning panel with exterior oil-based primer — PVC planks need no paint
  • Apply 2 coats of exterior latex paint to all wood trim — brush first, then light roller for an even finish
  • Apply your business name to the upper fascia panel — vinyl decal from a sign shop or hand-lettered paint
  • Install exterior chalkboard sign hooks or brackets on each side of the service window
  • Add interior shelving using 1×8 pine on adjustable shelf pin strip tracks — set to your display height
  • Install warm LED puck lights inside the upper shelf area — plug into a power strip on a timer for easy operation

Pro Tips

Hard-won advice to save you time, money, and frustration on build day.

⚖️
Weight distribution matters

PVC planks are light — great! But 2×4 corner posts add up. Keep heavier framing close to the trailer axle to avoid tongue-heavy or tail-heavy towing issues.

🪚
PVC cutting tip

Use an 80-tooth fine blade on your circular saw. PVC melts and gums up coarse blades. Cut slowly and let the blade do the work — don't force it.

📐
Blocking is not optional

PVC planks will bow and look wavy without solid backing every 16–24 inches. Add horizontal blocking between studs even where you think you don't need it.

📏
Standard counter height

Food cart counters are typically 34–36 inches from the ground. Measure your team's comfortable serving height before cutting the window opening — you can't move it later.

🏛️
Massachusetts permit heads-up

Even for a serving-only cart, Worcester requires a Temporary Food Establishment permit from the Board of Health. Apply early — it can take 4–6 weeks to process.

🌧️
Seal everything before rain

Do all caulking in one continuous session before the next rainfall. Exposed frame wood that gets wet before planking is installed can warp and throw off your whole alignment.

🔩
Dry-fit first, always

Before nailing any PVC planks, dry-fit an entire wall to confirm spacing and cuts. Far easier to adjust before anything is fastened permanently.

🛡️
Use stainless fasteners outside

Any nail or screw exposed to weather should be stainless steel — not galvanized, not zinc. Galvanized fasteners leave rust streaks on white PVC within a season.
